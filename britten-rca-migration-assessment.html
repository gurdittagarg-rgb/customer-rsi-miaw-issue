Britten — Modernizing Quote, Price & Order on Salesforce

From a custom 2014-era org to Revenue Cloud Advanced · FY27 engagement

1. What We're Looking At

Britten Inc. is a vertically integrated experiential branding leader — 250+ associates on a 500,000 sq ft Traverse City campus, serving the Super Bowl, Olympics, FIFA, Disney, Coca-Cola, MSG, and Big Ten programs through proprietary brands like BoxPop®, BannerSaver™, EventTruss™, and Foam3D™. In early 2026, Britten announced a strategic doubling-down on world-class event fabrication and expansion into Canada and Mexico.

Underneath that growth sits a Salesforce org built in 2014–2016 with Sales Cloud only. Over a decade, Britten's two-person Salesforce team has built a "Frankenstein" custom version of Revenue Cloud — custom Job/Job Item objects that mirror Order/Order Line, custom Invoice objects, and a robust Apex Print Estimator. It works, but only one of four divisions runs through it — the others are on spreadsheets. Newly released Salesforce features can't be adopted, Pipeline Inspector and forecasting are blocked, and 3,400 custom fields with no descriptions are now a real AI-readiness risk.

This assessment scopes a Revenue Cloud Advanced (RCA) modernization that lifts quoting, pricing, and the Job/Order layer onto standard Salesforce objects. Microsoft Business Central remains the financial system; Avalara, Authorize.net, and customer-portal integrations stay intact. Manufacturing floor screens and shipping middleware are untouched. The goal: a modern, admin-friendly foundation that scales to all four divisions and unblocks the growth Britten has already announced.

Why now: Britten is expanding into new geographies and divisions, but only one division can use the Salesforce platform end-to-end today. The custom code, the two-person team, and the AI-readiness gap are all compounding. Every quarter we wait, the cost to replace grows and the gap between divisions widens.

What We're Changing

  • Product catalog — attribute-based EPC catalog (no SKU explosion)
  • Pricing engine — replace the custom Apex/VF Print Estimator
  • Price tables — rates managed by admins, not developers
  • Quote experience — modern Lightning configurator & line editor
  • Order / Job — custom Job__c → standard RCA Order with decomposition
  • Invoice on Salesforce — custom Invoice__c → RCA Invoice (still feeds Business Central)
  • Pipeline & forecasting — standard objects unblock Pipeline Inspector

What We're Not Touching

  • Microsoft Business Central — remains the financial system of record
  • Tax (Avalara) — integration preserved, just repointed at RCA Invoice
  • Payments (Authorize.net) — unchanged
  • Manufacturing floor screens — pre-press, print queues, QR job sheets as-is
  • Customer-portal API integrations — preserved with light remapping
  • Subscriptions / recurring revenue — not part of Britten's model

2. Britten's Priorities — and the Challenges in the Way

A leadership view: what Britten is trying to achieve, what's blocking it, and how this engagement bridges the two.

Top 3 Priorities

1

Unify the Business on One Platform

Bring all four divisions — Print, BoxPop®, Foam3D™, Woodworking — onto a single, consistent Salesforce process, end-to-end. Make Salesforce the operational backbone of the company, not just one division.

Today: only the print division runs on Salesforce end-to-end. Newer divisions live in spreadsheets and standalone tools.

2

Fuel Growth Without Adding Friction

Support the announced expansion into Canada and Mexico and the doubling-down on event fabrication. Onboard new product lines, new geographies, and new fabrication offerings without a developer in the critical path.

Today: every new product variant or rate change is a developer ticket. The two-person team is a structural bottleneck on growth.

3

Become AI & Forecast Ready

Unlock Pipeline Inspector, Einstein, and Agent Force on the data Britten already has. Give leadership real-time forecasting and reps an AI assistant that actually works on Britten's data model.

Today: 3,400 undocumented custom fields block AI features and Pipeline Inspector. Britten owns the licenses but can't use the capabilities.

Top 3 Challenges to Overcome

A

A Decade of Custom Code

Apex classes, Visualforce pages, and a "Frankenstein" custom version of Revenue Cloud built since 2014. Custom Job__c, Job_Item__c, Invoice__c objects mirror standard ones — and need to be retired carefully.

What it costs: every Salesforce release passes Britten by. Newer divisions can't be onboarded because the custom build can't absorb their nuances.

B

A Two-Person IT Shop

Anthony and Colin maintain the entire org. The team has openly stated a preference for "admin-friendly" configuration and is hesitant to take on more dev work. Past partner experience was painful.

What it costs: any large rebuild lands on the same two people. Without the right partner approach, the project either stalls or burns the team out.

C

Sales & Fulfillment Are Disconnected

Reps stay close to deals after closure because process gaps force manual coordination. Reps chatter billing to confirm payment before delivery teams can start. Project Managers help but the gap is structural.

What it costs: reps spend cycles on operational coordination instead of selling. Customer-facing teams operate without real-time visibility into where a job actually is.

How RCA Bridges the Gap Revenue Cloud Advanced is a direct answer to all three priorities. Attribute-based catalog and admin-friendly Pricing Procedures retire the custom code and let the two-person team run the platform without writing more Apex (challenges A & B). Order Orchestration closes the post-sale visibility gap and pulls the other divisions onto a shared model (priority 1, challenge C). Standard objects with populated metadata unlock Pipeline Inspector and AI on day one (priority 3). And because Microsoft Business Central, the manufacturing floor, and payments stay untouched, Britten gets all of this with a tightly bounded blast radius — matching the team's appetite for safe, focused modernization.

Four divisions — only one fully on Salesforce

Print, BoxPop® (shipping containers), Foam3D™ (sculptures), and woodworking share one Salesforce process. But only print has a real pricing tool. Newer divisions live in "sophisticated spreadsheets."

4. Themes

What this project is really about.

1

Take pricing out of code

Admins update prices. Not developers.

Under the hood: Apex/Visualforce Print Estimator is replaced by a Pricing Procedure with admin-managed Price Lists. Constraint Builder enforces cost-plus bell-curve guardrails. Custom Apex actions cover only the truly bespoke math.

2

Build an attribute-based catalog

Match Britten's "everything is custom" mindset without a SKU explosion.

Under the hood: Product2 records in EPC with AttributeDefinitions and AttributeAssignments. Banner = vinyl/mesh/decal modeled as attributes, not 10K SKUs.

3

Custom Job → standard RCA Order

Retire Job__c / Invoice__c and migrate to Salesforce-standard objects.

Under the hood: Closed-Won creates an RCA Order with decomposition into Fulfillment Order Line Items. RCA Invoice replaces Invoice__c. BC integration repointed; Avalara repointed; manufacturing screens repointed where they read line-item data.

4

Unblock AI & forecasting

Pipeline Inspector, Einstein, and Agent Force become usable out of the box.

Under the hood: 3,400 undocumented custom fields are the AI-readiness blocker today. Standard RCA objects come with descriptions and metadata baked in — AI features start working without a separate cleanup project.

5

Microsoft Business Central stays put

Finance is undisturbed. So are payments, tax, and the production floor.

Under the hood: BC remains the system of record for finance. Avalara and Authorize.net integrations are repointed at RCA Invoice with a light field remap. Manufacturing screens (pre-press, print queues, QR job sheets) read from the new line items but keep their UX.

6

Close the post-sale visibility gap

Reps stop chasing billing; delivery teams stop waiting on chatter.

Under the hood: RCA Order Orchestration replaces ad-hoc handoffs with a configured fulfillment plan. Status flows automatically from "paid" to "ready to fulfill" — no manual chatter, no junction-object gymnastics for split shipments.

7

Prove prices match before go-live

Old and new systems priced side-by-side until they agree to the penny.

Under the hood: a parity harness replays 12+ months of historical quotes through both the legacy estimator and the new Pricing Procedure. Automated diff reporting; no go-live until parity is signed off.

8

One pattern, four divisions

Build right for print first — then bring BoxPop®, Foam3D™, and woodworking onto the same model.

Under the hood: EPC attribute-based modeling lets each division define its own attribute set against a shared Pricing Procedure framework. No four bespoke estimators. Sequenced as fast-follow phases.

9. What Could Go Wrong — and How We'll Manage It

New prices don't match the old prices Large

Banner pricing has been tuned over many years. There's a real risk the new system produces slightly different numbers if we miss a rule.

How we manage it: spend time up front documenting every pricing rule, then run thousands of past quotes through both systems and compare. We don't go live until they match.

Migrating Job__c to standard RCA Order is the riskiest piece Large

Job__c (~450 fields), Job_Item__c, Invoice__c, and their custom junction objects are deeply embedded. Moving them to standard RCA objects affects manufacturing screens, BC integration, and customer-portal APIs.

How we manage it: data-migration strategy is a partner-selection criterion (per the briefing note). Manufacturing UX stays put — only data sources change. Cut over by division so blast radius is bounded.

3,400 undocumented custom fields are an AI & analytics blocker today Medium

Forecasting and Pipeline Inspector don't work; Einstein and Agent Force can't reason over the schema. RCA on standard objects fixes this for the migrated layer — but the legacy fields remain.

How we manage it: the migration eliminates the bulk of the problem. Remaining legacy fields get a metadata-cleanup pass during phase 0 discovery, focused on what actually drives reports.

Reps don't take to the new screen Medium

The sales team is comfortable with today's tool. A new screen, even a better one, is a change.

How we manage it: keep the language and options familiar, pilot with a few reps first, and provide short in-app guidance.

Partner data sharing hits a snag Medium

The data feed to your long-term partner uses an older Salesforce feature that may not fully support the new line items.

How we manage it: validate it in a test environment early. If there's a gap, we drop in a lightweight integration to cover it.

Building the product list takes longer than expected Medium

You don't have a real product list today, so we're starting from scratch. That work can grow if scope isn't tight.

How we manage it: mine your past quotes to derive a starter list, then refine in workshops with sales and production.

Quote PDFs need a small refresh Easy

Your PDF templates were built against today's quote line items.

How we manage it: light template touch-ups during the build phase. Low effort, low risk.

Running old and new at the same time gets messy Medium

If we try to run both systems in parallel for too long, reps may quote the same banner two ways.

How we manage it: switch over one banner type or product family at a time, with a clean cutover for each. Avoid long dual-run.

A few reports need rebuilding Easy

Some line-item-level reports refer to the current data structure.

How we manage it: rebuild those reports against the new line items. Leadership dashboards barely change.

Britten's IT team is small Medium

Two people maintain the entire org, and one is part-time on Salesforce. A long migration on top of day-to-day work risks burnout or stalled progress.

How we manage it: joint-build with a recommended partner so Britten's team co-owns the design without carrying the full delivery load. Declarative-first to keep long-term maintenance manageable.

Scope creep into other divisions too early Medium

It's tempting to bring foam, woodworking, and shipping containers along in phase 1 since they're on spreadsheets today. That triples the work.

How we manage it: print division first as the proven pattern. Other divisions sequenced as fast-follow phases once the model is stable.
