Britten — RCA Mapping Workshop

Facilitator's Guide for the Architect Leading the Room

Your job in this workshop is not to teach RCA. Your job is to map Britten's current state to RCA capabilities, surface the right questions, and leave the room with shared confidence and a clear next step. You bring 10+ years of Salesforce judgment. Vandeep (RevCloud SE) brings deep RCA expertise. Anthony and Colin bring 15 years of Britten reality. Your value is the connective tissue between those three. This guide gives you the flow, the script, and the lifelines so you can lead with confidence even where your RCA knowledge is still building.

1. Your Mindset Going In

You are the orchestrator, not the SME.

Lead the room. Hold the agenda. Ask great questions. Defer RCA depth to Vandeep when needed — that is the design, not a failure.

Britten's language wins over RCA jargon.

Anthony talks in Jobs, Job Items, Estimator, Cost-Plus. Mirror those terms first, then bridge to RCA constructs. Don't make them learn your vocabulary on day one.

Whiteboard everything. Slides are background.

The fastest way to build trust is to physically draw their world on the wall and watch them correct you. Bring markers. Use them.

Anthony is wary of partners. Earn it slowly.

The 2014 implementation got scrapped. The "10-foot pole" architect anecdote is a real memory. Show your work, name your assumptions, invite pushback. Don't oversell.

  • Sync with Lily (AE) and Mitch (RevCloud AE). 15 min. Confirm desired commercial outcome of the workshop — is it scope agreement? Partner endorsement? Both? You facilitate to that outcome.
  • Send Britten a 1-pager pre-read. Include the assessment's "Priorities & Challenges" section and the "What We're Looking At" scope. Ask Anthony to flag anything wrong before the workshop.
  • Ask Anthony for one screenshot of the Print Estimator and one of a Job_Item__c record (with sensitive data redacted). Whiteboard from real screens, not abstractions.
  • Confirm partner attendance. Per the briefing note, the partner needs to demonstrate (a) data migration strategy and (b) Constraint Builder for cost-plus pricing. Make sure they know that's the expectation.
  • Decide format. In-person on Britten's campus is ideal. Whiteboard, sticky notes, 3 hours minimum.

Day before

  • Re-read the 4/8 and 4/23 meeting notes. Have 3–4 specific Anthony quotes in your head ("not with a 10-foot pole", "everything is custom", "two-man team"). Using their own words back to them is the fastest trust-builder.
  • Print the facilitator kit (Section 12 below).
  • Sketch the current-state architecture from memory on a piece of paper. If you can't, do it now. The first 20 minutes of the workshop is you drawing this on a whiteboard.
  • Pre-write the 3 questions you most want answered. Hide them at the bottom of your notes. If by hour 2 they aren't answered, ask them.

4. Segment 1 — Opening & Alignment (15 min)

Open the room

15 min You drive
Goal: set purpose, build psychological safety, get Anthony and Colin nodding before we go deeper.

Flow

  1. Welcome & intros around the room (1 min per person).
  2. State the purpose — one sentence (script below).
  3. Acknowledge what we already know about Britten's environment. Read back 2–3 specific things they told us so they know we listened.
  4. Ask: "What does a great outcome of today look like for you?" Capture their answers on the wall.
  5. Walk through the 3-hour agenda. Get explicit "yes" before moving on.
The reason we're here today is to map how Britten runs sales, jobs, and invoicing today onto Revenue Cloud Advanced — together. We're not pitching. We're not trying to convince you of anything. We're stress-testing whether RCA is actually the right fit for the way you work, and what would have to be true for it to succeed. By the end of today, we'll have a side-by-side picture of your current state and the RCA target state, a clear list of the hard questions, and three concrete next steps. If at any point something doesn't sound right, please push back — that's the most useful thing you can do for us.
Opening questions (capture answers visibly)
  • What does a great outcome of today look like for you?
  • What's the one thing you'd hate for us to miss?
  • Anything different from when you last spoke to Mitch and Lily that we should know?
Watch-out: don't let the opening become a re-explanation of RCA. Keep it short. The first hour belongs to them talking, not us.

5. Segment 2 — Confirm Current State (45 min)

They walk us through it. We whiteboard.

45 min Britten drives
Goal: have Anthony and Colin walk us through their world, end-to-end, while you draw it. By the end, the room shares one picture.

Flow

  1. Draw four boxes on the whiteboard: Opportunity → Quote → Job → Invoice.
  2. Ask Anthony to walk through what happens at each box. Add objects, automations, integrations as he speaks.
  3. Add Microsoft Business Central, Avalara, Authorize.net, customer-portal APIs as side boxes.
  4. Highlight the Print Estimator separately — this is the keystone object of the whole conversation.
  5. Ask the divisional question: "Where does this picture break for the other three divisions?" Capture differences in red.
Anthony, would you walk us through what happens from the moment a sales rep creates an opportunity all the way to when the customer pays? Use whatever language you'd use with a new hire. Don't translate to Salesforce-speak for our benefit. I'll capture it on the wall and stop you with questions as we go.
Probing questions to ask during their walkthrough
  • "Walk me through what the Print Estimator actually calculates." — force them to articulate the math.
  • "How do you update a substrate cost when it changes?" — surfaces the dev-dependency pain.
  • "What happens between Closed-Won and the production floor seeing the job?" — surfaces the Job__c trigger.
  • "When one quote line becomes multiple shipments, how do you handle that today?" — surfaces the junction-object workaround.
  • "What's the gap between print and the other three divisions?" — opens the unification priority.
  • "What happens when a rep needs to know if the customer paid?" — surfaces the chatter-billing problem.
  • "What do you wish worked differently today, regardless of RCA?" — gives you the customer's actual wishlist.
Whiteboard output: a single end-to-end diagram showing Opportunity, Quote, Print Estimator, Job__c, Job_Item__c, Invoice__c, Avalara, Microsoft Business Central, Authorize.net, customer-portal APIs, and the manufacturing floor. Mark in red any place where the current process breaks for the non-print divisions.
Watch-out: the temptation will be to start solving as they describe problems. Don't. Just listen and capture. Solutioning happens in segment 3.
Lifeline: if Anthony goes deep into a custom Apex class detail you don't follow, say "Can you show that to me on a screen at the break? I want to make sure I get it right on the wall." — this signals genuine interest without faking expertise.

6. Segment 3 — Map to RCA, Pillar by Pillar (60 min)

Six pillars, ~10 minutes each

60 min You facilitate, Vandeep narrates RCA
Goal: walk through each pillar of Britten's world and show the RCA equivalent. Get explicit "this works for us" or "this concerns us" for each.

How to run each pillar (10-minute pattern)

  1. You frame it (1 min): "Pillar 3 is product attributes. Today you have free-text fields on the Estimator UI."
  2. Vandeep narrates the RCA equivalent (3 min): with a real screenshot or short demo where possible.
  3. You ask the customer: "Does that map to how you think about it?" (1 min)
  4. Open discussion (4 min): capture concerns, questions, "how would this work for X" scenarios.
  5. You summarize (1 min): write the verdict on the wall — ✓ works, ? need to dig, or ✖ concern.

Lifeline: if Vandeep can't be present, you can still run this. For each RCA pillar, say "Vandeep can show this in detail in our follow-up. For now I want to confirm whether the concept maps to how you think about it." — defer the depth, lock in the concept.

7. Segment 4 — The Hard Questions (30 min)

Where Britten needs proof, not promises

30 min Partner + Vandeep
Goal: address the three things the partner briefing note explicitly called out as deal-makers. Get the partner on the floor for this segment.

Hard Question 1 — Constraint Builder for Cost-Plus Pricing (10 min)

Per the partner briefing note, this is non-negotiable. Britten needs to see Constraint Builder handle a cost-plus bell curve without custom code.

Anthony, the partner is going to walk through how Constraint Builder handles your cost-plus model. Pick the gnarliest rule you can think of from the Estimator. We'll try to model it live.

Hard Question 2 — Data Migration from Custom to Standard (10 min)

Job__c, Job_Item__c (~450 fields), Invoice__c, junction objects. The partner needs to show a credible migration approach.

Push hard on these
  • What's your sequencing? Which custom object goes first, which last?
  • How do we keep manufacturing operating during the cutover?
  • How do we handle in-flight jobs that exist on the day of cutover?
  • What's the rollback if we discover a parity break post-go-live?

Hard Question 3 — Pricing Parity Approach (10 min)

Banner pricing has been tuned over a decade. Mismatches at go-live = revenue and margin issues immediately visible to finance.

Lock in the parity approach
  • How many historical quotes will we replay? (target: 12+ months)
  • What's the acceptable variance threshold? (target: zero, then exception list)
  • Who signs off on parity? Anthony? Sales leadership? Finance?
Watch-out: if the partner can't answer one of these convincingly, that's data. Don't paper over it. Park it for follow-up. Britten will trust you more for naming the gap than for hiding it.

Surface what worries them

15 min You drive

We've covered a lot of ground. Before we close, I want to give Anthony and Colin five minutes to flag anything that's still nagging at them — the things that would keep you up at night about doing this. Big or small.
Direct prompts if the room goes quiet
  • What's the part of this you trust the least?
  • If we did this and one thing failed, what would you guess that one thing would be?
  • What would your reps say if we showed them the new screen tomorrow?

  • Is there anyone we haven't talked to whose buy-in we'd need?
Output: a "parking lot" list on the wall, each item with an owner and a date by which we'll come back with an answer.

11. Confidence Tactics for Moments You Don't Know

1. Defer with grace, not apology

Don't say "I don't know that." Say:

"That's a great question for Vandeep — let's pull him in for two minutes." or "I want to give you a precise answer rather than a guess. Let me come back with that by Friday."

2. Reflect their question before answering

Buys you 15 seconds, ensures you actually heard it, and often the customer answers their own question:

"So what you're really asking is whether RCA can model the cost-plus rule when there's no published list price — is that right?"

3. Use Britten's own examples

If you don't know how RCA handles something abstract, ask them for a concrete example first — then model that one example with Vandeep:

"Walk me through one specific banner job with all its options. We'll map that one end-to-end and see what we learn."

4. Anchor in your 10 years of Salesforce

You may be new to RCA, but you know the platform. Bring it up:

"In 10+ years on Salesforce I've seen plenty of orgs that look like yours — and the pattern of moving from custom objects to standard ones is well-trodden. The RCA-specific moves I'll lean on Vandeep for, but the migration shape I've done before."

5. Make the unknown a shared exercise

Some questions you genuinely can't answer in the room. Make discovering the answer part of the engagement:

"Honestly, I don't think any of us in the room can answer that without testing it. Let's add it to the parking lot and the partner will demo it back to us in two weeks."

6. Lean on Britten's built confidence

Anthony built a custom version of Revenue Cloud. He understands the problem space deeply. Surface that:

"Anthony, you've essentially built RCA by hand. You know better than most architects how this should hang together. Where do you think the seams are most fragile?"

7. Use silence

If you ask a question and nobody answers, do not fill the gap. Wait. Five seconds feels like five minutes, but the answer that comes after silence is usually the most honest one.

8. Capture, don't argue

If Anthony pushes back on something Vandeep says, your job is not to defend Vandeep. Your job is to capture the concern accurately on the wall.

"Got it — let me make sure I'm hearing that right. Your concern is X. Anthony, did I get that?"

12. Facilitator Kit (What to Print & Bring)

  • This guide, printed. Sections 4–9 (the segment cards) on one or two pages each.
  • The assessment HTML, printed. Especially the "Priorities & Challenges" section and the side-by-side comparison.
  • One sheet of A4 with the cheatsheet (Section 10). Folded in your pocket for quick lookups.
  • The pre-read you sent them. So you and they are looking at the same artifact.
  • Whiteboard markers in 3 colors. Black for current state, blue for RCA target, red for concerns / parking lot.
  • Sticky notes (two colors). One color for "concerns", one for "wins". Hand to Anthony and Colin during segment 5.
  • Phone with full battery. For photographing the whiteboard at the end.
  • Vandeep's phone number. If he's remote, dial him in for the hard questions even if not in the original plan.
  • Calendar open. When you propose the follow-up, send the invite from the room.

One last reminder. The customer chose to be in the room. They want this to work as much as you do. Anthony and Colin have already built a custom version of RCA — they're not skeptics, they're connoisseurs. Treat them as expert peers and the workshop will run itself. Your job is to keep the room oriented, surface the right questions, and leave with three concrete commitments. You've got this.
